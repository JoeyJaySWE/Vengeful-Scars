    A Guide to your own character sheet

    Intro - What is a character sheet
    If you are reading this, your probly thinking of making your own character sheet for one of your characters. That's great! But what is it good for, and why do you need one? Well, a character sheet contains all core abilities that make your character work and operate. It tells us GMs how good you are at taking a hit, dealing damage, avoid damage, and any other useful skill you might have in your arsenal. The sheet will help us determine how various actions affect your character, and help make out other useful things to be mind ful about your character. What is your characters current goal in life? Does it have any useful contacts? Does it have any distinctive personality trait that can give her an advantage/disadvantage in a social setting or combat? Be filling out these few sheets, you help us GMs saving ours of gussing how to best adjust a campagin to your prefered game style. If your character cant tell which way to point a blaster, or has deep fear of blood, maybe we won't have her going toe to toe with a rancor on her first day.

    Step 0 - Find your Race

    Go to page 278 in the REUP pdf Then look up the Race you are playing as since this will be deciding how many dice you can plays on attributes and Skills (Basically, your strengths and weaknesses). In the example below shows the data we're looking for.

    Languages: Add these langugages to the langugages section of your sheet.

    Attribute Dice: This is the number of dice you can add to your Attributes (Dex, Str, Perc, etc.). All player character (none NPCs) gets +6 additional dice.

    DEXTERITY 2D/4D: This shows the races minimum and maxium allowed Dice to assign to an attribute. In this example, you may not have less than 2D Dexterity, and not more than 4D.

    Move: 10/12 This shows the minimum and maximum of speed. You always start at 10 unless otherwise instructed by DM.

    Step 1 - Assign Attributes

    Attributes are like an inborn instinct that doesn't require any learning, in the words of the amazing Tod Howard "It just works!". E.g Perception, some people have the habillity to "just spot things" very well and be very good at spotting or notice small things or actions. This will be translated into a greater Perception attribute (4D6). Compared to a less perceptive / normal person (2d6).

    In accordance with your race data from page 278, start spending Attribute dice on points in Dexterity, Knowledge, Mechanical, Perception, Strength, Technical. Remember that you need to asign the minimum amount of dice to each Atrribute, to not exceede the maximum amount to each attribute, and that your total dice doesn't exceeded your races Attribute Dice +6.

    Step 2 - Assign Skill Points

    A skill point reflects a character expertice in a specific field of learning after a vast amount of time. You could compare this with taking a major at a University.

    Once you've spend all your Attribute dice it's time to add skills to your character. A list of skills you can pick you'll find at page 34 then you can find more detailed info of the skills on the following pages
    Note: Just because your character doesn't have any die assigned to said skill, it doesn't mean she can't use it. Only some skills in some situations requires training to use, that said, it's always good to keep your skills high. In case your lacking said skill, you're attribute dice will have to suffice.

    You get a total of 7 Skill dice to spend on skills. There are a couple of way to "cheat" the system to make it seem you have more skills.

    Transform 1 Skill Die to 3 Pips
    You can take one of your Skill Dice and transform it into 3 pips. Pips means that instead of getting an extra Die to roll, you only get a fixed number extra to your result. You can assign +1, +2 which are pips. This way you can have a skill Die in Brawling looking like "2D+1" meaning when you roll brawling you roll 2D6 and then add a +1 to the end result.

    Transform 1 Skill Die into 3 Specialized Dice
    You have a scoundrel that is an ace with a blaster, and such, you wanna add a skill Die to Dexterity -> Blasters. This is a good move as it helps your aim with blaster weapons, however, maybe your character only uses a specific type of weapons, like a Blaster Pistol ? Then he wouldn't need to have the same aim on a Blaster Rifle if he never uses them. Instead you can transform 1 Skill Die and say that instead of being good at all Blaster Weapons, you're specially good at "Blaster-Pistols". Meaning you get your bonus when firing your blaster pistols, but not if you use a blaster rifle. Then you have 2 more Specialized Dice you can spend before your Skill Die would be depleted.

    Note: You may not upgrade a specialized skill with the same skill Die. Meaning when you transform a Skill Die to a Specialized Die, you got 3 Specialized Dice. These must be assigned to different specialized skills. If you want to upgrade a specialized skill, you need to transform another skill Die. Think of the process like 1 Skill Die covering a major at university. The Specialized Die is like a course within said major. At year 1, you may take the 101 classes, but need to wait for year two for the next level of said class.

    Note: You can see what type of specializations you can pick for a skill on the skills detailed descriptions page. You may make more specializations together with your GMs if you can't find what your looking for.

    Advanced skills (optional)
    An advanced skill is a more difficult skill that requires a specific amount of pre-knowledge. These skills works differently from normal skills as they do not gain the attribute bonus, but do give their die code as a bonus to the prerequisite skills. Unlike normal Skills, Advanced Skills are supposed to be acquired over a long period of hard work. As such, they all have pretty high requirements on skills and points needed to be owned to unlock them. E.g. (A) Surgery requires a die code of 5D in First Aid in order to be able to assign 1D in (A) Surgery.

    Advantage / Disadvantage (optional)
    You may add a disadvantage to your character which will hamper your character somewhat when playing. Maybe you got a Drug Addiction for example, that would mean that you need to have your fix or suffer penalties to your stats. Doing this however, will give you a couple of bonus dice that you may add to your skills. On the other hand you could have an Advantage like Extraodinary Sight whish enables you to see 3x as far, but in exchange you suffer a -2D penalty to Skill dice. For a list of Advantages & Disadvantages see page 28.

    Always alert the GMs of any advantages / disadvantages your character may have, as it may effect events and gameplay significantly.

    Step 3 - Equipment

    On Page 2 of your Characters Sheet, there are a section where you might add Armor, Weapons and Equipment. All new character starts with 5,000 Credits to spend on those types of items. This section starts at page 355.
    Each pice of equipment, weapons, amor comes with a Item Availbality and Item Restrictions.

    Item Availbality is the general indication of how easily the equipment can be found, ranging from 1 (Readily Available throughout the known galaxy), to 4 (rare items to obtain anywhere).

    Item Restriction tells you whether the piece of equipment needs a license to own or operate, this is described with the letters F (fee), R (restricted) or X (illegal).

    Equipment
    May be tools or items you need in the field, like medpacks, progrmaming spikes, breath masks, ammo packs, etc. Many of these also requires some type of Skill to use (Like medpack requires First Aid ) If you don't have it, you would have a hard time figuring out how to use it properly. Make sure you meet the Skill Requirments and check if it's single use item or not.
    Note: Some of these may come in packs which can save you a great deal of money, make sure to check it out before buying.

    Weapons
    Sooner or later, you'll be hard pressed into a fight (pirate life ain't no pleasure cruise matey) As such it could be good too familiarise yourself with the weapons in the game.

    Melee Weapons
    Just like equipment, you need skills to utilize a weapon. Make sure to check the "Skill:" section of the weapons discription to see what skill they require. On your sheet, you then need to add the Weapon Type, Weapon Difficulty, and Weapon Damage.
    Weapon Difficulty shows how difficult it is for a player to use a weapon correctly and is rolled by your own character. If you succeed, you would hit the enemy, if you fail, you could hurt yourself. The Weapon Damage will show how many dice to roll when attacking.

    Ranged Weapons
    The range weapons type still has the Skill Requirement part, but they lack the difficulty from the melee weapons which is substituted for the Range you wanna fire from.
    Fire rate: shows how many attacks you can make with the weapon per round (most commonly 1)
    Range: the distances you may fire from (in meters). Short / Medium / Long range
    Damage: The dice you roll to deal damage

    Armor
    This is gonna hurt... or not, depending on your equipment and stats. Normally when receiving damage, you get to roll your Strength to avoid damage. Damage Dealt would then be reduced by the Damage Soaked from Strength + any bonuses applied by your armor. Be sure to note any possible penalities the armor may give you.

    Step 4 - Fill out Description

    Congratz, you now have all the basic knowledge you need to make your character. All you need to do now is fill out the background, contacts and other easier fields matching your characters description.
